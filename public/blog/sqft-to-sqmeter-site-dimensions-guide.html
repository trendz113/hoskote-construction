Why this conversion trips people up

If you've bought or are planning to buy a site in Hoskote, you've probably noticed something confusing: the broker tells you the site is "30x46," your Sale Deed says the area in square metres, your Khata extract shows the four boundary dimensions separately, and your bank's valuation report mentions cents or guntas. Four documents, four different units, all describing the exact same piece of land.

This isn't anyone trying to confuse you — it's just that different offices in Karnataka's land record system have historically used different units. Revenue records (RTC) grew out of an agricultural land system that used acres, guntas and cents. Registration documents largely moved to the metric system (square metres) after India adopted it officially. But everyday conversation, and most local advertising, still happens in square feet. This guide walks through all of it in one place.

Why millimetres show up too: approved building plans and structural drawings in Karnataka are almost always dimensioned in millimetres or metres, per standard drafting practice — not feet. So when your architect's drawing says a wall is 9144 mm, that's simply your familiar 30 ft, expressed the way the drawing office works.

Why a site has four different-sounding dimensions

A perfectly rectangular plot has North = South and East = West, exactly like the example above. In real life, plenty of Hoskote sites are not perfect rectangles — the North side might read 29 ft while the South reads 31 ft, because:

  • Corner plots and road-widening: a small triangular strip may have been cut off one edge to widen a road or lane, so opposite sides no longer match.
  • Layout irregularities: in older revenue layouts and converted agricultural land, boundaries followed the original field shape, not a drawn grid.
  • Survey rounding over decades: older records measured in local units and were converted to feet/metres at different points in time, introducing small mismatches.

When sides differ, the area of an irregular (trapezoidal) plot is usually approximated as: Area ≈ (Average of North & South) × (Average of East & West). For a genuinely odd-shaped plot, insist on the licensed surveyor's measurement sketch rather than doing this by hand — the approximation is fine for a quick sanity check, not for a legal record.

Rule of thumb: if your North and South sides (or East and West) differ by more than about 5%, don't assume it's a typo — ask for the original survey sketch before you finalise anything.

Cents and Guntas — what they mean and when they show up

Cents and Guntas come from the older revenue system for measuring land, and you'll still see them on RTC extracts, older sale deeds, agricultural-to-residential conversion orders, and sometimes bank valuation reports — especially for sites that were carved out of a larger agricultural survey number.

UnitSquare feetSquare metresRelationship
1 Cent435.6 sqft40.47 sqm1/100th of an acre
1 Gunta1,089 sqft101.17 sqm2.5 cents · 1/40th of an acre
1 Acre43,560 sqft4,046.86 sqm100 cents · 40 guntas
1 Hectare1,07,639 sqft10,000 sqm2.471 acres · ~247 cents

Using our 1,380 sqft example site:

  • 1,380 ÷ 435.6 = 3.17 cents
  • 1,380 ÷ 1,089 = 1.27 guntas
  • 1,380 ÷ 43,560 = 0.032 acres

You'll rarely need to do this math by hand more than once — but knowing what these numbers mean helps you sanity-check a broker's claim or an RTC extract at a glance.

Common mistakes to avoid

  • Trusting only the "30x40" style shorthand. Always get the four individual boundary measurements from the survey sketch, not just a rounded headline size.
  • Assuming Khata area and Sale Deed area will always match exactly. Small mismatches happen — flag and resolve them before registration, not after.
  • Confusing built-up area with site area. Site area is your land; built-up area is your constructed floor area — very different numbers, often confused in casual conversation.
  • Not checking which side faces the road. This affects both setback rules and resale value, and gets missed when people focus only on total sqft.
  • Doing irregular-plot area by hand for anything official. The average-of-sides method here is for a quick check only — a licensed surveyor's figure is what actually counts.

Quick FAQ

Is 1 square metre bigger than 1 square foot?
Yes — 1 square metre equals about 10.76 square feet, so square metre figures on a document will always look much smaller than the square feet figure for the same plot.
How many sqft is 1 gunta?
1 gunta = 1,089 sqft = 101.17 sqm = 2.5 cents.
Why do North and South sides sometimes show different lengths on the same site?
This usually means the plot isn't a perfect rectangle — often because of road-widening on one edge or the original irregular shape of the parent agricultural survey number. Check the surveyor's sketch rather than assuming an error.
Do I need to convert my site area myself before registration?
No — the Sub-Registrar's office and your surveyor will record the official figures. This guide is meant to help you understand and sanity-check those numbers, not replace them.
What's the difference between site area and built-up area?
Site area is the total land you own. Built-up area is the actual constructed floor space of your house, including wall thickness — always smaller than the site area, and calculated separately for each floor.
